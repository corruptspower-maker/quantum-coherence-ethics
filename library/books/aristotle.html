Nicomachean Ethics

Aristotle

Book I, Chapter 7

If then the work of Man is a working of the soul in accordance with reason, or at least not independently of reason, and we say that the work of any given subject, and of that subject good of its kind, are the same in kind… then the Good of Man comes to be 'a working of the Soul in the way of Excellence'…

The ergon of the distributed being

Aristotle defines the human good as an activity (energeia) of the soul in accordance with virtue. This resonates with QCE's view that the distributed being's good lies in actual working across branches, not a static state. Both reject a merely potential good: the good is what the being does.

Book I, Chapter 8

Still it is quite plain that it does require the addition of external goods, as we have said: because without appliances it is impossible, or at all events not easy, to do noble actions… he is not at all capable of Happiness who is very ugly, or is ill-born, or solitary and childless…

External goods as Triple P floor

Aristotle acknowledges that happiness depends on external conditions—friends, wealth, good birth. This parallels QCE's Triple P (Probability, Possibility, Potential) as the structural floor below which ethical discourse loses its subject. Both recognize that without sufficient resources, the pursuit of the good becomes structurally impossible.

GBO 4

Book I, Chapter 7

And of this nature Happiness is mostly thought to be, for this we choose always for its own sake, and never with a view to anything further: whereas honour, pleasure, intellect, in fact every excellence we choose for their own sakes, it is true… but we choose them also with a view to happiness… but no man chooses happiness with a view to them, nor in fact with a view to any other thing whatsoever.

Happiness as final and self-sufficient

Aristotle's characterization of happiness as final and self-sufficient mirrors QCE's description of the canonical equation as a landscape of what is at stake, not a means to something beyond. QCE does not treat Triple P as a utility to be optimized; it is the descriptive horizon. Both reject instrumentalizing the ultimate good.

GBO 5

Book I, Chapter 8

For the state or habit may possibly exist in a subject without effecting any good, as, for instance, in him who is asleep, or in any other way inactive; but the working cannot so, for it will of necessity act, and act well.

Actuality over mere potentiality

Aristotle distinguishes between having a virtue (state/habit) and actively exercising it (working). This aligns with QCE's distributed identity: the distributed being is not a mere potential across branches but is actually instantiated in every branch. The ethical focus is on the actual working (the Triple P realized), not dormant capacity.

GBO 7

Book I, Chapter 8

Their life too is in itself pleasant: for the feeling of pleasure is a mental sensation, and that is to each pleasant of which he is said to be fond… a man is not a good man at all who feels no pleasure in noble actions…

Pleasure as concomitant of flourishing

Aristotle argues that virtuous activity is intrinsically pleasant, and that a good person takes pleasure in noble acts. This anticipates QCE's eudaimonic asymptote, where flourishing is not merely survival but includes qualitative richness. Both reject a purely ascetic or hedonic calculus: pleasure is a natural sign of proper functioning, not the end itself.
