Enchiridion

Epictetus

I

There are things which are within our power, and there are things which are beyond our power. Within our power are opinion, aim, desire, aversion, and, in one word, whatever affairs are our own. Beyond our power are body, property, reputation, office, and, in one word, whatever are not properly our own affairs.

Dichotomy of control

This foundational Stoic distinction mirrors QCE's identification of Triple P (Probability, Possibility, Potential) as structural preconditions. Epictetus places survival-related externals (body, property) outside our power, while QCE treats Probability (P) as a gate that is largely not chosen. Both recognize a boundary between what the self can shape and what is given, but QCE extends this to the distributed being across branches.

GBO 2.2

V

Men are disturbed not by things, but by the views which they take of things. Thus death is nothing terrible, else it would have appeared so to Socrates. But the terror consists in our notion of death, that it is terrible.

Suffering as judgment

Epictetus argues suffering arises from subjective judgment rather than objective events. QCE challenges this by asserting that suffering is an ontological fact: the loss of a branch-self is real harm within that branch, and harm to others is structurally embedded in your own distribution (P2). The tension between Stoic cognitive therapy and QCE's realism about branching harm is central to the is/ought question.

GBO 2.3

XI

Never say of anything, 'I have lost it,' but, 'I have restored it.' Has your child died? It is restored. Has your wife died? She is restored. Has your estate been taken away? That likewise is restored. 'But it was a bad man who took it.' What is it to you by whose hands he who gave it has demanded it again? While he permits you to possess it, hold it as something not your own, as do travelers at an inn.

Restoration not loss

This passage resonates with QCE's concept of non-mortality: death ends a branch-self but not the distributed being. The Stoic discipline of treating possessions and loved ones as borrowed aligns with QCE's view that the present self is not the sole instance of the distributed being. Both discourage attachment to a single branch outcome, though QCE grounds this in the physics of branching rather than divine will.

GBO 2.4

LI

The first and most necessary topic in philosophy is the practical application of principles, as, We ought not to lie; the second is that of demonstrations as, Why it is that we ought not to lie; the third, that which gives strength and logical connection to the other two, as, Why this is a demonstration. ... But we do just the contrary. For we spend all our time on the third point and employ all our diligence about that, and entirely neglect the first.

Priority of practice over theory

Epictetus prioritizes practical application (the 'first topic') over meta-logical demonstration. QCE similarly refuses to derive ought from is (P7) and instead identifies the structural floor (Triple P) while leaving normative construction to individual action. Both frameworks emphasize that ethics is primarily about what you do with the given structure, not about proving the structure itself – a parallel to QCE's 'the normative building is yours to construct'.
