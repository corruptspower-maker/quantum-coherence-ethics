Chapter I

I had bewildered myself in metaphysics, and in theological controversy. Nothing else pleased me. History, and particular facts, lost all interest in my mind. ... Of providence, fore-knowledge, will, and fate, Fixed fate, free will, fore-knowledge absolute, And found no end in wandering mazes lost.

free will mazes lost

Coleridge's despair over the tension between free will and deterministic fate mirrors the classical philosophical problem that QCE resolves through branching reality. In MWI, every possible choice is realized, so the 'wandering mazes' are not lost—they are all real branches of the distributed being. This passage illustrates the cognitive need for a framework that accommodates multiple trajectories without contradiction.

Chapter II

The men of the greatest genius, as far as we can judge from their own works or from the accounts of their contemporaries, appear to have been of calm and tranquil temper in all that related to themselves. In the inward assurance of permanent fame, they seem to have been either indifferent or resigned with regard to immediate reputation.

This calmness mirrors the QCE principle of non-mortality: the distributed being's identity is not exhausted by any single branch-self. Chapter II

The sanity of the mind is between superstition with fanaticism on the one hand, and enthusiasm with indifference and a diseased slowness to action on the other. For the conceptions of the mind may be so vivid and adequate, as to preclude that impulse to the realizing of them, which is strongest and most restless in those, who possess more than mere talent... the latter must impress their preconceptions on the world without.

vivid conceptions and action

Coleridge distinguishes between those whose inner conceptions are so vivid they need no external realization and those who must shape the outer world. QCE resonates with the latter: the present self is an ancestor who shapes inherited conditions across all branches. The 'impulse to realize' aligns with the Triple P dimensions—probability, possibility, potential—as structural preconditions for ethical action. The mind's conception is a wave function of possible outcomes; action selects the conditions that propagate.

Chapter I

My earliest poems were marked by an ease and simplicity, which I have studied, perhaps with inferior success, to impress on my later compositions. ... I must be permitted to add, that, even at the early period of my juvenile poems, I saw and admitted the superiority of an austerer and more natural style, with an insight not less clear, than I at present possess.

poetic self across time

Coleridge reflects on the continuity of his artistic identity across different phases of his life, akin to the distributed being's persistence across branches. The early self's judgments remain present in the later self, just as present actions become ancestral gifts for downstream selves. This illustrates the ancestral structure: each self inherits and modifies conditions that shape subsequent expressions of the same distributed being.
