## GBO 6.1 — Preconditions Before a Norm Claim

The is/ought problem as Hume states it in *A Treatise of Human Nature*
(Book III, Part I, Section I): no chain of purely descriptive premises can
produce a normative conclusion without an unacknowledged logical step. You
can describe the world in exhaustive detail — its facts, its causes, its
consequences — and still not arrive at a single "ought" without smuggling
in a premise you haven't defended.

Hume noticed the gap. He did not close it. Most of Western ethics since
has either tried to close it by force (Kant's categorical imperative is a
machine for generating oughts from the structure of rational agency itself)
or accepted it and retreated into non-cognitivism (moral claims aren't true
or false, they're expressions of preference or emotion).

QCE takes a different path. The canonical threshold is not derived from
an is in the classical sense. It emerges from what suffering *is* across
all branches of a branching reality. In a universe where every quantum
event ramifies into real observer-containing branches, suffering is not
a preference or a convention — it is a structural feature of the ontology.
The observer who suffers in branch A is continuous with the observer who
does not suffer in branch B. Both are real. Both matter in exactly the
same sense that the original observer matters.

From this structure — not from preference, not from intuition, not from
convention — the canonical threshold appears. The ought is not derived
from the is. The ought *is* the is, seen from inside a branching universe
that contains observers who can suffer. Hume's gap does not close.
It dissolves. The question was never how to get from description to
prescription. The question was whether the description was complete.
It wasn't. It is now.
