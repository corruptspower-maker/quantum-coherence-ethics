Subject: Ethical framework built on Everettian ontology — connection to constructor theory

Dear Professor Deutsch,

My name is [NAME]. I'm a philosophy student and independent researcher working on an ethical framework grounded in the many-worlds interpretation — specifically, in taking MWI seriously as ontology rather than treating it as a computational convenience.

The framework is called Quantum Coherence Ethics. Its core claim is that a person is a distributed being — a single entity refracted across every branch of the wave function sharing its causal origin — and that ethical weight arises from the structural fact that present actions shape the statistical character of one's future distribution.

I'm writing to you for two reasons.

First, your work in *The Fabric of Reality* and *The Beginning of Infinity* — particularly your insistence that MWI is the only interpretation that takes quantum mechanics seriously, and your argument in the Dream of Socrates chapter that physics has moral implications — is the ground this framework stands on.

Second, I believe there is a specific connection to constructor theory. Constructor theory defines possibility with physical precision: a transformation is possible if a constructor exists that can perform it. QCE has a quantity — Possibility (Sigma) — that measures the scope of existence in a given branch. I suspect that constructor theory could provide the formal foundation for Sigma.

My question is this: **Does the constructor-theoretic distinction between possible and impossible transformations have implications for how we should think about ethics in a branching universe?**

I've attached a short formalization of the framework. I would be grateful for any response, however brief.
